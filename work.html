Selected Work

Building organizations. Leading transformation.

Twenty years building businesses, transforming global operations, and creating capabilities that didn't previously exist. The work below represents initiatives where strategy met implementation realitywhere understanding both organizational systems and human dynamics determined what actually became possible.

Discuss Your Challenge About My Practice

What This Work Taught Me

Leading transformation at scalewhether building new operations, reshaping organizational culture, or developing capabilities across thousands of professionalsrevealed patterns that frameworks rarely capture. The distance between strategic clarity and organizational reality. The way informal systems shape what's actually feasible. How change unfolds through people, not just through structure.

Strategy Meets Implementation

The most elegant strategic designs encounter messy human realities. Success required holding both simultaneouslythe analytical rigor to think through implications and the organizational understanding to know what would actually work in specific cultural contexts.

Building While Operating

Creating new capabilities while maintaining existing operations demands different judgment than pure startup work or pure optimization. You're managing stakeholder expectations, integrating into established culture, and delivering results before the new capability is fully mature.

Scale Changes Everything

Leading change across thousands of people in multiple countries surfaces challenges invisible at smaller scale. Communication cascades differently than intended. Cultural nuances shape how initiatives land. What works in one geography requires thoughtful adaptation elsewhere.

The Long Arc of Change

Meaningful organizational transformation unfolds over years, not quarters. Early wins matter for momentum. But sustained change requires patience with the pace at which new ways of working become embedded in how people actually operate day to day.

Selected Projects

Each initiative below required navigating organizational complexity at scalebuilding new capabilities, reshaping culture, leading transformation across diverse stakeholder groups. The challenges were less about technical execution and more about understanding how change actually unfolds in human systems.

01 Establishing BCG's Global Capability Center

Leading the establishment and growth of BCG's India-based Global Capability Centerbuilding an operation from inception to serve the firm's worldwide client work. The challenge wasn't simply operational setup. It was creating an integrated capability that maintained BCG's quality standards while building an entirely new talent model and operating approach.

Building from Zero

Establishing the GCC meant defining what didn't existnew role profiles, hiring criteria that balanced technical capability with cultural fit, operational processes that integrated with global teams across time zones and working styles. Each decision set precedents for what would become permanent organizational infrastructure.

The Talent Challenge

Hiring for roles we'd never previously defined, in a market where BCG's GCC model was new. Success required understanding both the technical capabilities needed and the human dynamics of how these teams would collaborate with colleagues worldwidebuilding credibility and integration from day one.

Cultural Integration

The GCC couldn't operate as a separate entity. It needed to function as an integrated part of BCG's global systemunderstanding firm culture, building relationships across geographies, establishing trust with senior stakeholders who initially questioned whether this model could maintain quality standards.

02 Developing BCG's Employee Value Proposition

Leading the development and implementation of BCG's global Employee Value Propositionarticulating what the firm offers its 30,000 professionals and why someone would choose to build their career here. The work required engaging senior stakeholders worldwide, understanding diverse perspectives on what BCG meant to different populations, and translating insight into sustainable practice.

Stakeholder Complexity

Engaging senior leaders across regions, practice areas, and functionseach with legitimate but often competing perspectives on what mattered most. Success meant creating space for genuine dialogue while moving toward decisions. Understanding when to push for alignment and when divergent views reflected real differences that the EVP needed to accommodate.

Global, Yet Local

An EVP that worked globally while respecting regional realities. What resonates in New York differs from what matters in Singapore or So Paulo. The work required understanding these nuances without losing coherencearticulating a value proposition that felt authentic across vastly different contexts and career stages.

From Articulation to Practice

The greater challenge wasn't articulating the EVPit was embedding it in how the organization actually operated. Translating aspiration into talent decisions, development programs, and daily management practice. Ensuring the gap between stated values and lived experience didn't quietly undermine credibility over time.

Senior Influence Without Authority

Leading this work meant influencing senior stakeholders who held formal authority over their regions and practices. The work required building coalitions, demonstrating value through early wins, and maintaining momentum when progress felt slow. Knowing when to escalate and when to work through informal channels.

03 Leading BCG's Analytics & Product Business

Building and leading a global team of 300+ analytics and product professionalscreating a new capability that didn't exist when I started. The work combined P&L accountability with the challenge of integrating a fundamentally different talent model into BCG's established consulting culture.

Building a New Practice

Launching an analytics practice meant creating what didn't existdefining role profiles for data scientists and product managers in a consulting context, building hiring processes, establishing career paths. Each decision set precedents for how this capability would develop over years.

Global Coordination

Managing teams across continents, time zones, and cultural contexts. Building operational cadence that kept people aligned without creating bureaucracy. Ensuring knowledge sharing happened naturally across geographies while respecting local market realities and client relationships.

Cultural Integration

Analytics professionals think differently than traditional consultants. They value different things, work at different rhythms, define success through different measures. The challenge was building a team that maintained its distinct identity while integrating seamlessly with case teams worldwide.

P&L Accountability

Full responsibility for business performancerevenue, profitability, resource allocation, investment decisions. Making calls about where to grow aggressively and where to pull back. Managing the tension between building long-term capability and delivering quarterly results.

Talent Development at Scale

Growing from a handful of people to 300+ professionals required different approaches at different stages. What worked when the team was 30 people didn't scale to 100. Building systems that developed talent consistently across geographies while preserving the human relationships that make development actually work.

Client Value Creation

Ensuring analytics capability translated into client impact. Not just technically sophisticated work, but solutions that addressed real business problems and could be implemented successfully. Bridging the gap between what's analytically possible and what's organizationally feasible.

04 Executive Coaching Practice

Providing executive coaching to senior leaders navigating the challenges that come with operating at the highest organizational levels. The work focuses less on skills development and more on thinking through complexityhelping leaders develop their own judgment on decisions that resist standard frameworks.

What makes this effective is the combination of coaching methodology with direct experience leading similar initiatives. I understand the territory these leaders are navigating, not just theoretically but from having made comparable decisions under similar constraints.

The Nature of the Work

Most coaching engagements center on specific challenges leaders are working throughorganizational restructuring with political complexity, strategic decisions where multiple valid paths exist, leadership transitions requiring different capabilities than what brought them to this level. The work is concrete, focused on what they're actually facing.

Holding Strategic & Human Dimensions

Effective coaching at senior levels requires engaging with both the analytical substance and the human dynamics. The strategic logic of a decision. The organizational politics that shape feasibility. How to navigate stakeholder relationships while maintaining forward movement. Both matter; neither alone is sufficient.

Pattern Recognition Across Contexts

Twenty years building businesses and leading transformation created pattern recognition that informs the coaching. Not prescriptive adviceeach situation has distinct characteristics that matter. But understanding the recurring dynamics that show up when scaling operations, building new capabilities, or leading change through established organizations.

05 Leading Organizational Restructuring

Serving as Head of People for a nonprofit organization navigating fundamental restructuringnot incremental adjustment, but rethinking organizational design, reporting relationships, and how work gets done. Leading this initiative meant holding both the analytical work of organizational design and the deeply human work of helping people through significant change.

Structural Redesign

The analytical work of restructuringunderstanding current state, identifying structural inefficiencies, designing new operating models. Making decisions about spans of control, reporting relationships, role definitions. Ensuring the new design addressed real operational challenges, not just theoretical improvements.

The Human Dimension

Restructuring affects people's livesjob security, career trajectory, daily working relationships, sense of purpose. The work required holding space for legitimate anxiety while maintaining forward momentum. Understanding when to push through resistance and when resistance signaled problems with the design itself.

Nonprofit Complexity

Nonprofits operate under different constraints than corporationsboard governance structures, mission-driven cultures, resource limitations, stakeholder complexity. Solutions that work in corporate contexts often need significant adaptation. Understanding these dynamics shaped both the restructuring design and implementation approach.

This work reinforced what I'd learned through corporate transformationthat successful reorganization is as much about understanding human systems as organizational design. The most analytically sound restructuring fails if it doesn't account for how change actually unfolds through relationships, informal power dynamics, and the pace at which people can genuinely adapt to new ways of working.
